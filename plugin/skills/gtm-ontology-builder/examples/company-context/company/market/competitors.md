# Competitor landscape

Every vendor in this document is fictional. The profiles illustrate how a public
example can store company-level competitive context without asserting facts about
real organizations.

## Fictional competitor archetypes

### Metric Harbor

- **Example domain:** `https://metric-harbor.example`
- **Category:** self-service commerce reporting
- **Strength:** quick setup and a low entry price for standard storefront dashboards
- **Tradeoff:** limited metric governance and few activation workflows
- **Typical fit:** smaller brands replacing spreadsheets and native platform reports

### Signal Foundry

- **Example domain:** `https://signal-foundry.example`
- **Category:** warehouse-native customer activation
- **Strength:** flexible audiences and broad downstream destination coverage
- **Tradeoff:** assumes a mature warehouse, stable identity model, and available data engineers
- **Typical fit:** data-led companies that already operate a governed warehouse stack

### Commerce Atlas

- **Example domain:** `https://commerce-atlas.example`
- **Category:** enterprise customer data and analytics suite
- **Strength:** broad capabilities, global services, and enterprise procurement support
- **Tradeoff:** longer implementation, higher cost, and more platform administration
- **Typical fit:** large organizations consolidating several customer-data capabilities

## Company-level comparison

Acme Analytics differentiates through commerce-specific definitions, progressive
adoption, and a shared model spanning analysis and activation. Detailed win/loss
guidance belongs in each product group's positioning artifact because competitors,
buyers, and evaluation criteria differ by group.

## Maintenance rule

When adapting this structure for a real company, replace fictional profiles with
dated, attributable research. Record what was observed, when it was verified, and
which product group the comparison affects.
