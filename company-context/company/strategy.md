# Company strategy

## Mission

Help digital commerce teams make first-party customer data useful without forcing
every team to build and maintain its own analytics infrastructure.

## Target-market choices

Acme Analytics serves digital commerce organizations that control first-party
transaction or customer data and perform recurring analytics or activation work.
The company does not target every organization with customer data. Each product
group selects a narrower customer situation, use case, champion, and current
alternative.

Commerce Analytics is the established entry point for commercial teams that need
governed recurring analysis. Data Activation is a selective expansion for
data-mature organizations that already operate a warehouse and need governed
delivery into business systems.

## Portfolio posture

The company is in an expansion phase: preserve a repeatable Commerce Analytics
motion while validating Data Activation with a narrower technical and operational
readiness bar. A new product group is not added until an existing group has a clear
segment, accountable champion, repeatable use case, bounded offer, and owned GTM
motion.

The portfolio uses product groups to separate customer situations that require
different champions, buying evidence, product capabilities, or routes to market.
Plans within one group package different levels of scope or service; they do not
create a new market merely because their limits or price differ.

## Shared narrative

Acme Analytics sits between basic storefront reporting and fully custom data
platforms. It provides opinionated workflows that are faster to adopt than a
bespoke stack while preserving the governance, exports, and integration paths
required by data-mature organizations.

This is a company-level narrative boundary, not a universal product positioning
statement. Product-group positioning must name its own segment, use case, current
alternative, market maturity, and differentiated product truth.

## Strategic choices

1. Serve defined commerce use cases instead of becoming a general-purpose BI tool.
2. Package capabilities around distinct buying contexts and product groups.
3. Make trustworthy definitions and traceable data more important than dashboard volume.
4. Support progressive adoption, from a managed analytics workspace to governed activation.
5. Integrate with existing commerce, warehouse, and engagement systems rather than replace them.

## GTM constraints

- Each product group has one primary segment and one primary motion at a time.
- Demand generation may educate adjacent organizations, but qualification still
  requires evidence of the workflow, problem, ownership, and operational readiness.
- Positioning is validated against the selected segment before it drives broader
  product or channel expansion.
- Offers must preserve bounded onboarding and explicit customer responsibilities;
  growth cannot depend on unpriced custom implementation.
- Claims stay close to observable product capabilities. Revenue, cost, or risk
  outcomes remain hypotheses unless the company has evidence and controls the
  material dependencies.

## Decision horizon

Portfolio and GTM choices are reviewed on a 12-month operating horizon. Market
claims, segment assumptions, and differentiated capabilities are reviewed earlier
when customer evidence, product availability, or competitive alternatives change.

## Shared differentiation

- Predefined commerce metrics and workflows shorten time to value.
- Business users can explore governed data without writing queries.
- Data teams retain control through documented definitions, exports, and access rules.
- Product groups share a common data model while addressing different buyers and motions.

Group-specific segments, positioning, value propositions, messaging, and proof
belong in each product group, not in this company-wide artifact.
